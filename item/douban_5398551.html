Shaun Tan was born in 1974 and grew up in the northern suburbs of Perth, Western Australia. In school he became known as the 'good drawer' which partly compensated for always being the shortest kid in every class. He graduated from the University of WA in 1995 with joint honours in Fine Arts and English Literature, and currently works full time as a freelance artist and author in Melbourne.

Shaun began drawing and painting images for science fiction and horror stories in small-press magazines as a teenager, and has since become best known for illustrated books that deal with social, political and historical subjects through surreal, dream-like imagery. Books such as The Rabbits, The Red Tree, The Lost Thing and the acclaimed wordless novel The Arrival have been widely translated throughout Europe, Asia and South America, and enjoyed by readers of all ages. Shaun has also worked as a theatre designer, and worked as a concept artist for the films Horton Hears a Who and Pixar's WALL-E. He is currently directing a short film with Passion Pictures Australia; his most recently published book is Tales from Outer Suburbia.

    Language Fictional languages are woven throughout the illustrations in the The Arrival, to the extent that it’s not entirely correct to call it a wordless story. The main recurring script is completely arbitrary yet hopefully convincing: a surgical rearrangement of Roman letters and numbers, using scissors and tape. The effect is immediately disorientating, which in itself reminds us of our dependence on the written word for security and authority when it comes to meaning. This fake-alphabet experiment confirmed my decision to abandon other text-based narrative ideas, including some handwritten letters sent home from husband to his wife and child. (Although we still see these letters, I felt they were far more evocative when left unreadable.) This also meant that I did not have to worry about the problem of an English-speaking immigrant, as I wanted to avoid any culturally specific characters. More importantly, the use of incomprehensible language places us, as readers, in the mind of the protagonist. We are the new arrival, only able to decipher meaning and value from visual images, object relationships and human gestures, and then only by making creative associations. Imagination here is more useful than any knowledge. When other communications fail, my character resorts to sketching objects in a notebook that he carries everywhere: drawings of a room, a loaf of bread, even an abbreviation of his homeland in simple outline. It’s no coincidence that the main character in The Arrival looks like me: I used myself as a reference model out of convenience, but there’s also a self-reflexive comment here on the role of an illustrator as storyteller, where some ideas can only be expressed through a silent language of images.
    语言
    虚构的语言贯穿在《抵岸》这本书的插画里，某种程度上说，把这本书叫做无字书并不是完全的正确。主要的，重复出现的文字是完全随心所造的，但是它很有可能令人信服：一种用剪刀和带子剪裁、重新整理后的罗马字母、数字。这立即产生了一种让人不知所谓的效果。这效果本身提醒我们，在涉及意义时，我们是依靠文字来获得安全和权威的。这个生造字母的实验使我更加坚定了我决定放弃其他基于文字叙述的想法，包括一些丈夫寄给妻子和孩子的手写家书。（我们仍然看到这些信，虽然它们不能阅读却能很大程度的唤起我们的感情。）
    这也意味着我不必担心一个说英语的移民者带来的问题，因为我想避开任何文化特性。更重要的是，对读者来说，使用难懂的语言可以让我们进入到主角的思想中。因为刚刚来到，所以只能够从这些可见的图像，对象的关系和人们的姿势中辨认出意思和用途，而且这只能靠有创造性的联系才能做到。想象力在这里比知识更加有用。当其他的交流失败了，我的主角会拿出他随身带着笔记本画出事物的草图：用一些简单的轮廓画出一间房，一片面包，甚至他家乡的缩写词。
    《抵岸》里的主角看起来像我并不是巧合：出于方便我把自己作为一个参考的模型，但是这也是关于叙述者，同时也是插画师的自我反省式的评论，其中的一些想法只能通过一种安静的图像语言来表达。

    Models The modeling of creatures and interiors is part of a more general approach I take when it comes to developing ideas. I try to use anything that might be helpful in visualising each scene, including photo-collages and small paper construstions, more or less expanding the concept of ‘sketching’ to encompass other media. The scene of towering giants destroying a city is a typical example. The initial concept was inspired by a description of communism by a Romanian refugee: she explained it as an ideology of negation, of liberty and beauty being ‘sucked away’ and replaced by a spiritless pall of ‘silent greyness’. For me, this provoked quite a literal mental image: powerful beings sweeping a world with vacuum cleaners (also suggestive of that terrible euphemism ‘ethnic cleaning’). To test this idea as a visual composition, I assembled cardboard boxes on my studio floor- a small ‘city’-and borrowed my dad’s work overalls and garden pesticide sprayer. I then took photographs of myself ‘menacing’ a tiny world illuminated by floor-level desk lamps. The process felt faintly ridiculous, but did produce an interesting result: a series of images that evoked a feeling of weird horror, nor unlike a 1950s science fiction story. I edited my photographs digitally, replaced the cardboard city with images of Florentine architecture, printed this out and began drawing over the top of it. After several redrafts, and in spite of its initial absurdity, the image developed some semblance of a ‘real’ event, reminiscent of Nazi book burnings and industrialized genocide. Many other scenes for the book involved crude props in a similar way, usually to overcome a ‘block’ when concepts seemed difficult to grasp through sketching alone, or simply to study the effects of light and shadow, which is instrumental for realistic illustration. Ultimately, I was trying to create a seamless experience for the reader as well as myself, a sense of something real and convincing, but also playfully constructed.

    Models The modeling of creatures and interiors is part of a more general approach I take when it comes to developing ideas. I try to use anything that might be helpful in visualising each scene, including photo-collages and small paper construstions, more or less expanding the concept of ‘sketching’ to encompass other media. The scene of towering giants destroying a city is a typical example. The initial concept was inspired by a description of communism by a Romanian refugee: she explained it as an ideology of negation, of liberty and beauty being ‘sucked away’ and replaced by a spiritless pall of ‘silent greyness’. For me, this provoked quite a literal mental image: powerful beings sweeping a world with vacuum cleaners (also suggestive of that terrible euphemism ‘ethnic cleaning’). To test this idea as a visual composition, I assembled cardboard boxes on my studio floor- a small ‘city’-and borrowed my dad’s work overalls and garden pesticide sprayer. I then took photographs of myself ‘menacing’ a tiny world illuminated by floor-level desk lamps. The process felt faintly ridiculous, but did produce an interesting result: a series of images that evoked a feeling of weird horror, nor unlike a 1950s science fiction story. I edited my photographs digitally, replaced the cardboard city with images of Florentine architecture, printed this out and began drawing over the top of it. After several redrafts, and in spite of its initial absurdity, the image developed some semblance of a ‘real’ event, reminiscent of Nazi book burnings and industrialized genocide. Many other scenes for the book involved crude props in a similar way, usually to overcome a ‘block’ when concepts seemed difficult to grasp through sketching alone, or simply to study the effects of light and shadow, which is instrumental for realistic illustration. Ultimately, I was trying to create a seamless experience for the reader as well as myself, a sense of something real and convincing, but also playfully constructed.

    Random sketches can often yield a ‘menu’ of shapes and ideas that becomes a useful reference throughout a project. Here, the vague theme is ‘objects’, and I tried to think of strange equivalents to every common device, from clocks to mailboxes. I was also mindful of a similarity between man-made objects and the plants and animals of the ‘new country’, as if designers here have taken their cue from nature. Once simple surrealist device that I like to use involves the enlargement of small ordinary objects: traffic cones as towering beacons, mechanical flywheels the size of buildings; a single leaf for a tree, tilting like a solar panel towards the sun. Because drawings don’t have an inherent scale, it’s relatively easy to imagine such transformations.

    Lodgings Throughout history, migrants have often sought to escape poverty, only to find it has merely followed them to another shore. The quality of accommodation for newly arrived immigrants can be the lowest of the low: cramped, run-down, unclean and poorly serviced; hidden garrets in an otherwise affluent metropolis. The apartment rented by my own character is at least functional and fully furnished, although it’s un clear what those furnishings are exactly, or how different appliances are meant to be used. There are three minimal rooms-a kitchen, a washroom and a small bedroom loft only accessible by ladder- each with a practical, narrative purpose. To this end, I constructed rough approximations of these rooms in my own home using cardboard boxes, simple wooden frames and furniture second-hand stores, in order to understand how these spaces might look, function and be illuminated. By the end of the book, more objects have accumulated in my character’s loft, giving the impression that the man has been living there for some time. The creature of the apartment, originally hiding in the small washroom, has had his ‘nesting pot’ moved upstairs as winter sets in. Another animal begins raising a family on an out door window ledge which seems specifically designed for this purpose. I was interested here in broad concepts of home, migration and nesting within a city; the idea of a place that does not draw great distinctions between the needs of nature and humanity.
